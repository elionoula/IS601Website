Dot Com Bubble

The Dot Com Bubble, also known as the internet bubble, was a rapid rise in U.S. technology stock equity valuations fueled by investments in internet-based companies during the bull market in the late 1990s. The age of ecommerce had been transformed with new technologies like eBay and Amazon. Amazon was birthed in a garage in Seattle by Jeff Bezos, who started out on Wall Street. Amazon was launched in summer of 1995 around the same time Pierre Omidayar was launching eBay, an auction site on the web.

Measure of success was immediate for Amazon and eBay because they both were selling tons of items upon release. Early success of these companies spread to Wall Street because the companies started putting up great numbers quarter after quarter. Upon the creation of the internet it took only 4 years before it had 50 millions users. This exponential growth was attributed to two key factors, Moore’s Law & Metcalfe’s Law. Moore’s Law is defined as the principle that the speed and capability of computers can be expected to double every two years, as a result of increases in the number of transistors a microchip can contain. Metcalfe’s Law is defined as the value of a telecommunications network is proportional to the square of the number of connected users of the system.

In the 1990’s tons of internet companies (Yahoo, Excite, Amazon, etc) went public. Bezos followed the mantra “get big fast” because it would allow them to help customers at a faster rate. Public key cryptography was an instrumental creation during this time period because it allowed for secured ecommerce. The public key infrastructure can be defined as a key cryptographic technology, which is widely used in open network-based E-commerce systems. As a foundation for E-commerce security, it enhances the security service functionalities, such as authentication, confidentiality, integrity, and non-repudiation of the systems.

Meg Whitman was brought into eBay to show Wall Street that the company was legit company for them to invest in. The day eBay launched, the price rocketed from $15 per share to $50 per share. Henry Blodget said although Amazon was posting huge losses that the share prices would double, he issued a statement saying the price would reach $400 in the next year. Amazon’s stock price doubled in a few short weeks which would mark the start of the internet bubble. Blodget would later be banned by the SEC because incriminating emails were found from him where he highlighted the mass amount of internet companies going public were not good companies.

The IPOs of Amazon and eBay started a craze of people to create new internet companies in an attempt to create similar riches that Jeff and Pierre had achieved. Tons of people wanted to get involved in internet companies which spawned the rise of day traders who would buy stocks in the morning and sell them later in the day for profits. Wall Street and venture capitalists understood that lots of the new companies that were coming up were not valuable, but they did not want to sit by while companies started making money and were banking that at least one of the companies would survive and thrive.

Fiber optics also contributed to the bubble because tons of telecommunication companies were attempting to provide people with larger bandwidth to operate at quicker frequency. In 1999 the total amount of internet companies to go public was 250, this was more than the previous high which was 30 companies in 1995.

Wall Street believed the holiday season would bring huge gains to ecommerce companies, and when they didnt they began to doubt the “get big fast” mindset that Jeff Bezos had been preaching. In April 2000 the internet economy brought the nasdaq to fall 355 points, known as black friday. Looking back at the Dot Com Bubble people viewed it as a good thing because it paved the way for the internet being scaled and expanded via better bandwidth.
